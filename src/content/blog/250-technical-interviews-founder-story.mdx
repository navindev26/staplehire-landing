*What sitting on the interviewer side for years taught me about how broken the hiring process really is.*

For several years, as a Senior iOS Developer at a startup, interviewing wasn't my job — it was something I did on top of my job. Whenever the company needed to grow the technical team, engineers like me got pulled into the process. You review a resume, hop on a call, ask some questions, give your verdict, and get back to your actual work.

Over five to six years, I conducted somewhere between 250 and 300 technical interviews. Out of all of those, roughly 50 candidates moved on to the next round.

That's about 1 in 5. And the more I did it, the more I realised how much of that outcome came down to luck — not the candidate's, but ours.

## What Nobody Tells You About Being the Interviewer

When you're a developer conducting interviews, there's no real training for it. You're handed a role description, maybe a rough idea of what to look for, and then you're on your own.

So you do what feels natural. You ask the questions that would have challenged you. You probe the areas you personally care about. You make a judgment call based on how the conversation felt.

The problem is that every other engineer on the panel is doing the exact same thing — just with different questions, different priorities, and different standards. Two interviewers could speak to the same candidate on the same day and come away with completely opposite reads, not because the candidate was inconsistent, but because they were never evaluated on the same things.

I saw this happen constantly.

![Every engineer on the panel asks different questions — so two interviewers can score the same candidate completely differently](/landing/research-flow.gif)

## The Signal Was Terrible

After enough interviews you start to notice patterns in your own process — and they're not flattering.

Candidates who were confident and articulate often advanced further than candidates who were technically stronger but less polished in conversation. People who happened to have worked with the same tech stack I had got the benefit of the doubt in ways others didn't. First interviews on a Monday morning were evaluated differently than ones on a Friday afternoon, even if nobody admitted it.

None of this was intentional. It was just what happens when the process has no real structure. The bar moved depending on who was interviewing, what mood they were in, and what they happened to prioritise that day.

And yet the company was making permanent hiring decisions based on this.

## 200 Rejections That May Not Have Deserved It

Here's what stayed with me. Out of 250+ interviews, about 200 people didn't make it past me to the next round. Some of those were clear — wrong skill level, wrong fit, obvious mismatches.

But a meaningful number of them? I'm genuinely not sure. They might have been strong candidates who just didn't get asked the right questions. Or who interviewed on a day when I was deep in a deadline and not at my sharpest. Or whose strengths happened to fall outside the narrow set of things I thought to probe.

That's not a small thing. These are real people whose shot at a role may have come down to an inconsistent process they had no control over.

## The Bigger Point

The interview process at most startups isn't broken because people don't care. It's broken because it's been bolted onto the jobs of people who have other things to do — engineers, team leads, founders — without any real structure to hold it together.

AI doesn't replace the human judgment that matters in hiring. But it can replace the inconsistency, the variance, and the noise that makes the whole process so unreliable in the first place.

After 250 interviews, I'm convinced that's exactly where it needs to start.
